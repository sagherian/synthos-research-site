In plain English

Boston Scientific makes the small, life-saving devices doctors put inside your body — heart-rhythm zappers and mapping catheters (its fast-growing FARAPULSE ablation system), the WATCHMAN implant that lowers stroke risk, stents, and tools for urology, pain and cancer. It is a big, profitable, steadily growing company: sales rose about 20% last year and it keeps roughly 17 cents of every sales dollar as profit.

Here's the strange part: the stock has fallen about 57% in a year even though the business kept growing and management recently raised its forecast. That means one of two things — either the market got scared about something that hasn't hit the numbers yet (tougher competition, a lawsuit, a pipeline setback), or investors simply overpaid a year ago and the price is snapping back to earth. Because the business itself still looks healthy, we think today's price of about $45 is cheap — you're paying only ~13× next year's expected profit for a company that grows double digits. Our verdict is Buy, but tactically — a bargain-hunting position you size small and add to carefully, not a bet-the-farm holding, precisely because a fall this steep usually means the crowd knows something.

Here's what our three scores mean in everyday terms:

  • Downside Risk 5/10 (middle of the road). The company is financially solid (low debt, a calm-moving stock) and the price is already cheap — but a crash this big is itself a red flag.
  • Growth Quality 7/10 (good). A high-quality, reliably growing business with fat margins — just not a rocket ship.
  • Exponential Potential 4/10 (below average). It should keep growing, but it's already a $67B company in a mature market, so don't expect it to multiply quickly.

The one big worry: a 57% drop is the market shouting. If it's right — if a rival's heart-ablation device is winning, or a legal or pipeline problem is coming — the cheap price could get cheaper. The whole call depends on the next earnings confirming the business is still fine.

1. What it is

Boston Scientific (NYSE: BSX) is a global medical-technology company founded in 1979, headquartered in Marlborough, Massachusetts, with ~53,000 employees. It designs and sells interventional medical devices across three franchises, now reported in two segments. Fiscal year ends December 31.

Revenue mix (FY2025, from filings):

  • By segment (product): Cardiovascular $13.27B (66%) · MedSurg $6.83B (34%). Total $20.07B. Cardiovascular (interventional cardiology, structural heart / WATCHMAN, peripheral, and the Cardiac Rhythm & Electrophysiology unit that houses FARAPULSE PFA) is the growth engine — Q1'26 Cardiovascular grew 13.5% reported / 11.2% organic vs MedSurg 7.8% / 5.7% (endoscopy, urology, neuromodulation).
  • By geography (FMP tag): United States $12.86B (64%) · Non-US $7.21B (36%). Q1'26 growth was broad — U.S. +10.9%, APAC +14.7% reported, EMEA +10.1% reported, LACA +19.0% reported. A US-weighted but globally diversified base.

The strategic engine is category leadership in electrophysiology — the FARAPULSE Pulsed Field Ablation (PFA) platform for atrial fibrillation, whose ADVENT long-term data (presented at ACC 2026) showed better four-year AF success and fewer repeat ablations vs thermal ablation — plus the WATCHMAN FLX left-atrial-appendage closure device, whose CHAMPION-AF study supported a first-line stroke-prevention use. Growth is supplemented by a steady cadence of tuck-in acquisitions (e.g. Valencia Technologies, closed Q1'26).

4. Exponential Potential

Synthos separates compounders (durable high returns on capital) from exponentials (accelerating, multi-baggers-from-here). BSX is a solid compounder that is decelerating, not an exponential:

  • Forward growth: revenue CAGR FY25→FY30E ~8.8% ($20.1B → $30.6B est); adjusted-EPS CAGR ~14% (~$2.72 FY25 → ~$5.20 FY30E) as margins and mix improve. Real, but mid-single-to-double-digit, not explosive.
  • Acceleration (the 2nd derivative) is negative: reported revenue growth ran +20% in FY25 and management now guides ~7.0–8.5% reported / 6.5–8.0% organic for FY26. The FARAPULSE-led inflection already happened; from here BSX decelerates toward a high-single-digit organic grower. Per our flagship philosophy we favor forward accelerators — BSX is on the wrong side of that second derivative.
  • Room to run: the interventional-cardiology/EP/structural-heart TAM is large but mature and contested — Medtronic, Abbott, J&J and Stryker all compete directly. At $67B the cap is far from the law-of-large-numbers ceiling, so "room" is not the binding constraint; growth rate is.
  • Reinvestment runway: healthy — FY25 FCF $3.66B, capex only ~4% of sales, and the M&A engine (Valencia and others) recycles cash into adjacencies. But acquisition-built growth (goodwill+intangibles are 58% of assets) is lower-quality than organic and carries integration/impairment risk.

Exponential Potential: Moderate-Low (4/10). Own BSX for durable ~10%-ish compounding and a possible valuation re-rate, not for a fast multibagger.

5. Financials (real numbers — FMP annual/quarterly)

  • Revenue: FY25 $20.07B, +19.9% (FY24 $16.75B, +17.6% on FY23 $14.24B). A rare ~20% growth print at $20B scale — driven by Cardiovascular and the FARAPULSE ramp.
  • Quarterly trajectory: Q1'25 $4.66B → Q2 $5.06B → Q3 $5.07B → Q4 $5.29B → Q1'26 $5.20B (+11.6% YoY reported, +9.4% organic). Growth is moderating from the 2025 peak but still solidly double-digit reported.
  • Margins: gross 70.4% TTM, EBITDA margin 27.5%, operating ~20%, net 17.2% TTM and improving (net was 11.1% FY24). Solid device economics; note GAAP margins are depressed by heavy amortization of acquired intangibles.
  • Earnings: GAAP net income $2.89B FY25 (EPS $1.96 / $1.94 diluted), up from $1.85B. Adjusted EPS FY25 ~$2.72; management guides FY26 adjusted EPS to $3.34–$3.41. Q1'26 GAAP EPS $0.90 (boosted by a tax benefit); adjusted $0.80 vs $0.75 a year ago.
  • Cash flow: operating CF $4.53B, capex −$0.88B, FCF $3.66B FY25 (up from $2.65B) — a ~5.1% FCF yield on today's cheaper price. Cash conversion is strong.
  • Balance sheet: total debt $12.4B, cash $2.0B, net debt $10.4B, net-debt/EBITDA ~1.7× — investment-grade (FMP letter rating B+) and easily serviced (interest coverage ~11.6×). No dividend; cash goes to M&A and deleveraging.

6. Valuation — priced in or room?

On forward earnings, BSX is cheap for its growth: 13.4× FY26E adjusted EPS, 12.1× FY27E, 8.7× FY30E, EV/EBITDA 13.5×, EV/S 3.7×, FCF yield ~5.1%. A ~9% revenue / ~14% adjusted-EPS grower with 70% gross margins would ordinarily command a high-teens-to-low-20s forward multiple — MedTech peers like Stryker and Abbott trade richer. The PEG-style read is favorable: 13× forward earnings against a ~14% EPS CAGR is a sub-1 growth-adjusted multiple.

The honest counterweight is the chart. A stock does not fall 57% in a year without a reason, and the FMP data set gives us the symptom (price, multiple compression) without the cause. Either (a) the market overpaid a year ago — BSX at ~$108 was ~40×+ forward earnings, a genuine bubble multiple that has simply normalized — or (b) something structural (PFA competitive share loss, a legal/regulatory or pipeline shock) is being priced ahead of the numbers. Our base-case ~$67 splits the difference: well above today's $45 (crediting the cheap multiple and intact fundamentals) but below the Street's $82.4 (discounting for the unexplained severity of the decline). Street targets (context): consensus $82.4, high $120, low $55 — 39 Buy / 5 Hold / 0 Sell. Not a value trap on the numbers we can see; but a value setup that requires the next print to confirm the business is intact.

8. Moat & competitive position

BSX's moat rests on (1) category leadership in high-growth niches — FARAPULSE PFA is an early leader in the fast-shifting AF-ablation market, and WATCHMAN dominates left-atrial-appendage closure; (2) a broad, sticky interventional portfolio sold into hospitals with entrenched physician relationships and training switching costs; and (3) scale in R&D and M&A that lets it keep buying and integrating adjacencies. The vulnerability is that its best growth markets — especially PFA — are exactly where the largest MedTech competitors are pouring resources. The de-rating may reflect fear that PFA becomes a share/price war rather than a BSX monopoly.

Peer set (market cap): Medtronic $106B (the closest direct comp), Abbott $166B, Stryker $125B, Danaher $140B, Amgen $202B, Gilead $163B, Pfizer $139B, McKesson $92B, HCA $91B, Sanofi $104B. Within pure MedTech, Medtronic, Abbott and Stryker are the relevant competitors; BSX trades at a lower forward multiple than Stryker/Abbott despite comparable or better recent growth — the crux of the value case.

9. Management, capital allocation & guidance

  • Capital allocation: growth-oriented — no dividend; free cash flow (~$3.66B FY25) funds a steady stream of tuck-in acquisitions (Valencia in Q1'26) plus deleveraging (net-debt/EBITDA ~1.7×). The heavy goodwill/intangible base (58% of assets) is the cost of this M&A-led strategy and a source of GAAP-margin drag and impairment risk.
  • Insider activity (the notable tell): in May 2026, three directors bought stock on the open market — Ludwig, Habiger and Pegus — at ~$56–$57, meaningfully above today's $45. Open-market director purchases (not option exercises) are a genuine positive signal of insider confidence at prices near the recent range. CEO Mahoney made a gift (estate planning, not a sale) and the CFO had routine RSU-vesting/tax withholding — nothing alarming.
  • Management's own guidance (half-weighted — their own book): in the Q1'26 earnings release (SEC 8-K, 2026-04-22), management beat its own Q1 guidance (11.6% reported sales vs a 10.5–12.0% range; adjusted EPS $0.80 at the top of the $0.78–$0.80 range) and raised/affirmed the full year: FY26 net-sales growth ~7.0–8.5% reported / 6.5–8.0% organic and adjusted EPS $3.34–$3.41; Q2'26 sales growth ~5.5–7.5% reported and adjusted EPS $0.82–$0.84. This is management's self-interested framing (half-weight), but it directly contradicts the idea that the business is deteriorating — the fundamentals guided up while the stock fell. Treat as supportive but not dispositive.

11. Key risks

  • The unexplained −57% drawdown (the dominant risk): the market is pricing something the reported fundamentals do not yet show. If it is right — competitive PFA share loss, a pipeline miss, or a legal/regulatory shock — the cheap multiple can compress further. We can see the symptom, not the full cause.
  • Competitive intensity in PFA/EP: BSX's best growth market is where the biggest MedTech players are investing hardest; category leadership is not guaranteed.
  • Acquisition-built balance sheet: goodwill + intangibles are 58% of assets; a large impairment would dent GAAP earnings and book value.
