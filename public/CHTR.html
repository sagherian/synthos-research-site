In plain English

Charter is the company behind Spectrum — the cable, home-internet, mobile phone and TV service that about 32 million US households and businesses use. It makes a lot of cash, but it is not growing: revenue actually shrank about 1% last year, and it is slowly losing internet customers to fiber and to cell-phone-based home internet.

Here's the unusual part: the stock is astonishingly cheap — around 3.6 times earnings, when a typical stock trades near 20 times. It has fallen about 67% in a year. Charter uses its cash to buy back its own shares aggressively, which means each remaining share owns a bigger slice of the company every year. If the business just holds roughly steady, that shrinking share count alone can push the stock up.

Our verdict is Buy — Tactical: a bargain worth a small, opportunistic position, not a safe long-term anchor. The company carries a mountain of debt (~$97 billion), so if customers keep leaving, the bargain can stay a bargain — or get worse.

Here's what our three scores mean in everyday terms:

  • Downside Risk 7/10 (elevated). Cheap, yes — but heavy debt and a business that's shrinking make a stumble dangerous. The low price already reflects fear.
  • Growth Quality 4/10 (below average). The underlying business isn't growing; the earnings-per-share gains come from buybacks, not from selling more.
  • Exponential Potential 3/10 (low). This is a value/turnaround bet, not a rocket. Don't expect it to multiply — expect it to recover if debt gets paid down.

The one big worry: faster internet from fiber companies and cell carriers keeps stealing Charter's customers, so the cash cow shrinks before Charter can pay down its debt.

1. What it is

Charter Communications (NASDAQ: CHTR), operating under the Spectrum brand, is the second-largest US cable operator, serving ~32 million customers across 41 states. Its network passes ~58.7 million homes and businesses. The business is a broadband-and-connectivity utility: sell high-speed internet over an owned coaxial/fiber plant, bundle mobile (an MVNO riding Verizon's network plus its own CBRS spectrum), and monetize a declining video/voice base on top. Fiscal year ends December 31. CEO is Christopher Winfrey; HQ in Stamford, CT.

Revenue mix (FY2025, from FMP product segmentation):

  • Residential $42.58B (78%) — of which Residential Internet $23.77B (the profit engine, 43% of total), Residential Video $13.70B (declining), Residential Mobile $3.76B (the one real grower), Residential Voice $1.35B.
  • Commercial (small business + enterprise) $7.32B (13%)
  • Advertising $1.47B (3%)

Geography: 100% United States — FMP provides no geographic segmentation because there is effectively one geography. The concentration is a US-cable-market bet, full stop.

The two swing factors buried in the mix: Internet (flat-to-down as fiber and fixed-wireless take share — Q1'26 internet customers fell 120k YoY) and Mobile (+17% lines YoY, the growth offset). The strategic story is convergence: bundle mobile with broadband to slow churn, finish a multi-gigabit network evolution (targeted 2027) and a subsidized rural buildout, then let capex roll off and free cash flow inflect.

4. Exponential Potential

Synthos separates compounders (durable high returns on capital) from exponentials (accelerating, multi-baggers-from-here). CHTR is neither — it is a deleveraging value/turnaround:

  • Forward growth: revenue CAGR FY25→FY30E is ~0.2% ($54.8B → $55.3B on consensus) — essentially flat. This is not a growth company.
  • Acceleration (the 2nd derivative): negative-to-flat. Revenue went +2.4% (FY22) → +1.1% (FY23) → +0.9% (FY24) → −0.6% (FY25), and Q1'26 revenue fell 1.0% YoY. The business is decelerating into contraction, not accelerating.
  • The EPS "growth" is buybacks, not operations. Diluted share count fell from 209M (2020) to ~127M (Q1'26) — a ~39% reduction — and consensus EPS rises from ~$41 (FY26E) to ~$65 (FY30E) almost entirely because the denominator keeps shrinking while net income is roughly flat. Honest framing: own this for the per-share math, not the business.
  • Room to run: the US broadband TAM is mature and contested — fiber overbuilders (AT&T, frontier) and fixed-wireless (T-Mobile, Verizon) are actively taking share. There is no large untapped runway; the runway is defending the base.

Exponential Potential: Low (3/10). The only thing "exponential" here is the compounding effect of the buyback on a shrinking share base — a financial lever, not a demand curve. Correctly sized as a value satellite, not a growth holding.

5. Financials (real numbers — FMP annual/quarterly)

  • Revenue: FY25 $54.77B, −0.6% (FY24 $55.09B, +0.9% on FY23 $54.61B). Flat-to-down at scale — the defining fact.
  • Quarterly trajectory: Q1'25 $13.74B → Q2 $13.77B → Q3 $13.67B → Q4 $13.60B → Q1'26 $13.60B (−1.0% YoY). Video revenue is the drag (−9% YoY in Q1'26); mobile service revenue +15% is the offset.
  • Margins: gross ~43% TTM, EBITDA ~37% ($21.2B FY25), operating ~24%, net ~9%. Margins are high and stable — this is a cash machine, just not a growing one.
  • Earnings: net income $4.99B FY25 (attributable; $5.77B before minority interest), diluted EPS $36.21. TTM net-income-per-share ~$39.3 (FMP). Q1'26 diluted EPS $9.17.
  • Cash flow: operating CF $16.08B FY25, capex −$11.66B (elevated: network evolution + rural), FCF $4.42B. FCF yield on market cap ~21% — the crux of the value case. Note capex is near a peak; the bull case is it rolls off after 2027.
  • Balance sheet (the risk): total debt $97.1B, net debt $96.6B, net-debt/EBITDA ~4.7×. Current ratio 0.40. Thin equity ($16B) inflates ROE to ~31%. Interest expense ~$5.0B/yr eats a third of operating income (interest coverage ~2.6×). This is an investment-grade-but-stretched, rate-sensitive capital structure.

6. Valuation — priced in or room?

CHTR is statistically cheap on almost every cash-based metric: 3.6× trailing EPS, EV/EBITDA 5.7×, EV/sales 2.1×, price/FCF ~4.8×, FCF yield ~21%, P/B 1.05×. On the forward estimates the P/E is 3.3× (FY26E) → 3.0× (FY27E) → 2.1× (FY30E) — but note that compression is driven by the buyback shrinking share count, not by the business growing.

The honest question isn't "is it cheap?" (it is) but "is it a value trap?" The market is applying a ~3.6× multiple because it doubts the durability of the cash flows: flat revenue, shrinking internet subs, $97B of debt, and secular fiber/FWA competition. A re-rating to even 5–6× EPS — still cheap — would be a double, which is why the bull case is so wide. But if EBITDA erodes while leverage stays >4.5×, the "cheap" multiple is simply correct.

Street targets (context): consensus $225.25, high $437, low $125 — a spread so wide it confirms low analyst agreement. Our $185 base-case FV is below consensus because we weight the secular erosion and leverage more heavily than the average sell-side model. Not a quality-compounder buy; a deep-value / deleveraging buy where the margin of safety is the price itself.

8. Moat & competitive position

Charter's moat is a capital-intensive owned network passing ~58.7M premises — a genuine barrier (nobody rebuilds a national cable plant cheaply). But the moat is shrinking at the edges: (1) fiber overbuild — AT&T, Frontier and municipal fiber are passing Charter's territory with symmetrical multi-gig, structurally superior to coax; (2) fixed-wireless access — T-Mobile and Verizon are adding home-internet subs on 5G at low prices, taking the value end of the market; (3) the video bundle is in secular decline (cord-cutting), removing a historical retention hook. Charter's counter is convergence (Spectrum Mobile +17% lines, MVNO economics improving with owned CBRS spectrum) and a network-evolution upgrade to symmetrical multi-gig by ~2027. The bet is that owned-network + mobile bundling defends the broadband base long enough for capex to fall and debt to amortize.

Peer set (market cap, from FMP): the FMP peer list is mostly international telecoms (Chunghwa Telecom $34B, Telefónica $21B, Vodafone $30B, Rogers $17B, Telus $16B, TLK $14B) plus Fox $25B and Liberty Broadband (LBRDA) $4.6B — Charter's largest shareholder and a de-facto tracking stock. The most relevant domestic comps (Comcast, fiber overbuilders, T-Mobile/Verizon FWA) are not in this particular FMP list but define the competitive reality. CHTR at ~$19B market cap / ~$116B EV is the largest pure-play cable name here.

9. Management, capital allocation & guidance

  • Capital allocation: the defining feature. Charter runs a buyback machine — $5.13B of stock repurchased in FY25, share count down from 209M (2020) to ~127M (Q1'26). No dividend. Debt is held roughly flat (net issuance +$1.0B FY25) while FCF funds buybacks. At a 3.6× multiple, buying back stock is arguably the highest-return use of cash — if the business holds. The risk: buying back a levered, declining-subscriber business can destroy value if the equity is a melting ice cube.
  • Insider activity (mixed but notable): the most interesting signal is a director open-market purchase — Mauricio Ramos bought 9,929 shares at ~$141 on 2026-05-15 (a rare insider buy into the collapse). Offsetting it, Director Emeritus Thomas Rutledge (former CEO) sold his remaining ~88k shares at ~$144–147 in late May. Liberty Broadband (10% owner) shows "D-Return" share movements tied to its merger/exchange mechanics, not open-market selling. Net read: one fresh insider conviction buy, one legacy-holder exit — no clean signal.
  • Management's own guidance (the earnings-call track, half-weighted — they talk their book): the Q1'26 release (SEC 8-K, 2026-04-24) is a real earnings release and carries CEO Chris Winfrey's forward framing: "we are poised for improving customer and free cash flow growth" as Charter completes its rural and network-evolution initiatives (network evolution targeted for completion in 2027). Management explicitly frames the thesis as: finish the heavy capex, then let free cash flow inflect. Concrete Q1'26 datapoints they lead with: mobile lines +1.8M over LTM (12.1M total), internet customers −120k in the quarter, Adjusted EBITDA $5.6B (−2.2% YoY), FCF $1.4B, $963M of buybacks. No explicit numeric full-year revenue/EBITDA guidance range is given in the release — it is a results-plus-narrative document. Treat the "FCF inflection after 2027" as management's self-interested framing, half-weighted.

10. Catalysts & what to watch

  • Next earnings: 2026-07-24 (Q2'26; Street EPS $10.10, revenue ~$13.52B). The line that matters: internet net adds (is the −120k Q1 bleed stabilizing?) and mobile line growth.
  • Capex roll-off: the single biggest FCF swing — network evolution is targeted to complete in 2027. Every quarter of falling capex directly lifts FCF and accelerates the buyback/deleveraging.
  • Leverage trajectory: net-debt/EBITDA moving toward 4× (bull) vs stuck >4.5× (bear). Rate moves matter given ~$5B annual interest.
  • Competitive data: fiber-overbuild pace in Charter's footprint and FWA net-add momentum from T-Mobile/Verizon.
  • Liberty Broadband: the LBRDA merger/exchange mechanics affect the share count and float.

Thesis tripwires (what would change the call): two more quarters of accelerating internet-sub losses; EBITDA turning down more than ~2–3% YoY; leverage rising instead of falling; or the buyback being cut to defend the balance sheet (would remove the core return lever).

11. Key risks

  • Secular broadband share loss (structural, the big one): fiber overbuild + fixed-wireless are structurally superior/cheaper at the margins; if internet subs keep falling, the cash-cow shrinks before debt is repaid. This is a value trap risk, not just volatility.
  • Leverage: ~$97B debt, 4.7× net-debt/EBITDA, current ratio 0.40, ~$5B/yr interest, coverage ~2.6×. Rate-sensitive and unforgiving if EBITDA slips.
  • Buyback-dependent thesis: the equity return leans on continued aggressive repurchase; any pause (to protect the balance sheet) removes the main upside lever.
  • Video/voice runoff: ~$15B of declining revenue still in the mix; the decline is a persistent headwind even as internet/mobile grow.
  • No expert corroboration: zero Synthos KB coverage — no independent high-skill voice has underwritten this, so conviction is structurally low.
  • Wide-outcome / low-agreement: Street targets span $125–$437; this is a high-dispersion situation where being wrong is costly.

Buy — Tactical. CHTR is a genuine deep-value / deleveraging setup: ~3.6× earnings, ~21% FCF yield, and a share count collapsing ~8–10%/yr, priced for a business the market has largely given up on. Our base-case fair value ~$185 (+35%) sits below Street consensus precisely because we take the secular erosion and leverage seriously — the upside is real but it is a re-rating-and-buyback bet, not a growth compounder, and the −67% chart plus death-cross posture mean the tape is against you today.
