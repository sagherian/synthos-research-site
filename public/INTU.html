In plain English

Intuit makes TurboTax (the app millions use to file taxes), QuickBooks (the bookkeeping software most small businesses run on), Credit Karma (free credit scores and loan offers), and Mailchimp (email marketing). These are products people use every year and rarely switch away from.

The business itself is doing fine — sales are still growing double digits and it throws off enormous cash. But the stock has been cut by two-thirds in a year, from about $800 to $275, because investors are scared that artificial intelligence will do your taxes and bookkeeping for free, making Intuit's products less special. So today you can buy a very profitable, still-growing company for a cheap price — about 10 years of earnings, versus 20+ for most software.

Our verdict is Buy, but only as a smaller "satellite" position — a contrarian bet, not a cornerstone. The reward is real if the fear is overblown; the risk is real if it isn't.

Here's what our three scores mean in everyday terms:

  • Downside Risk 5/10 (middle of the road). The company has almost no debt and gushes cash, which is safe — but the stock has already fallen hard and the AI worry is genuine, so it can still hurt.
  • Growth Quality 8/10 (very good). This is a top-tier, highly profitable business that keeps growing and keeps most of every sales dollar.
  • Exponential Potential 4/10 (modest). It grows steadily but it won't double overnight — the payoff here is the price recovering, not the company suddenly exploding in size.

The one big worry: if AI assistants can genuinely do taxes and bookkeeping for free, Intuit's ability to charge premium prices weakens — and that's exactly what the market is betting on right now.

1. What it is

Intuit (Nasdaq: INTU) is a ~40-year-old financial-software company (IPO 1993) headquartered in Mountain View, CA, run by CEO Sasan Goodarzi. It sells financial-management, payments, compliance and marketing software across four segments. Fiscal year ends July 31.

Revenue mix (FY2025, from FMP segmentation):

  • Global Business Solutions (QuickBooks + Mailchimp): $11.08B (59%) — the growth engine and the profit core.
  • Consumer (TurboTax): $4.87B (26%) — seasonal, high-margin, the most AI-exposed line.
  • Credit Karma: $2.26B (12%) — consumer fintech / lead-gen, rebounding (+32% YoY on the segment).
  • ProTax (Lacerte/ProSeries/ProConnect for accountants): $0.62B (3%) — small, sticky, professional.
  • By geography: FMP provides no geographic segmentation for INTU; the business is overwhelmingly US-centric, which concentrates both tax-policy and macro exposure.

The strategic story management is telling is "AI as friend, not foe" — embedding generative-AI agents ("Intuit Assist") across QuickBooks and TurboTax to raise the value of the platform. Whether that offsets or accelerates commoditization is the entire bull/bear axis of this note.

4. Exponential Potential

Synthos separates compounders (durable high returns on capital) from exponentials (accelerating, multi-baggers-from-here). INTU is a high-quality compounder, not an exponential:

  • Forward growth: revenue CAGR FY25→FY30E ~13.2% ($18.8B → $35.0B est); EPS CAGR ~14% on the non-GAAP series ($23.8 FY26E → $40.4 FY30E est).
  • Acceleration (the 2nd derivative) is roughly flat-to-negative: revenue growth was +15.6% FY25, and consensus implies ~+13.5% FY26E → +11.6% FY27E, easing toward low-teens. There is no growth inflection here — this is a steady double-digit compounder, not an accelerant.
  • Room to run: the SMB-accounting and consumer-tax categories are large and Intuit is already the dominant incumbent — which means the TAM is more about deepening ARPU (Credit Karma, payments, payroll, AI agents) than a virgin market to conquer. At $75B the law of large numbers plus a mature category caps the multibagger.
  • The honest reframe: the real upside optionality here is not growth acceleration — it's a valuation re-rating. The stock fell from ~$800 to $275 on multiple compression, not on collapsing fundamentals. If the AI fear proves wrong, the payoff comes from the P/E going back up, which our bull case captures.

Exponential Potential: Low-Moderate (4). Own it for cheap, durable ~14% earnings compounding plus a re-rating call option — not for a fast multibagger. This is a value/quality thesis, which is precisely why it's a satellite, not a core exponential.

5. Financials (real numbers — FMP annual/quarterly)

  • Revenue: FY25 $18.83B, +15.6% (FY24 $16.29B, +13.4% on FY23 $14.37B). Consistent mid-teens growth at scale.
  • Trailing-twelve-months (through Q3'FY26, Apr-2026): revenue ~$20.9B (Q4'25 $3.83B + Q1'26 $3.89B + Q2'26 $4.65B + Q3'26 $8.56B). Q3 is the giant tax-season quarter — note the extreme seasonality (Q3 alone is ~41% of the year).
  • Margins: gross 81.0% TTM, EBITDA 33.6%, operating 27.5%, net 21.9% TTM. Best-in-class software economics.
  • Earnings: FY25 GAAP net income $3.87B, EPS $13.82 (diluted $13.67), up from FY24 $2.96B / $10.58. (The forward estimates and earnings-beat figures cited elsewhere are the higher non-GAAP series, which excludes stock comp and amortization — non-GAAP is what the Street models. FY25 stock-based comp was $1.97B, ~10% of revenue: a real, watch-it dilution cost.)
  • Cash flow: FY25 operating CF $6.21B, capex just −$124M (asset-light), FCF $6.08B — a 10.2% FCF yield at today's price. This is the number that anchors the value case.
  • Balance sheet: total debt $6.64B against $4.55B cash + investments → net debt $3.76B; net-debt/EBITDA just 0.3×. ROE 23.3%, ROIC 16.2%, interest coverage ~16×. Financially unimpeachable.

6. Valuation — priced in or room?

For a company with 81% gross margins and a 10% FCF yield, INTU is cheap on every forward measure: 16.6× trailing GAAP EPS, 11.6× FY26E, 10.0× FY27E, 6.8× FY30E (non-GAAP), EV/EBITDA 11.0×, EV/sales 3.7×. Software of this quality has historically traded at 25–35× earnings; INTU spent years above 30×. The stock now sits at roughly one-third of its prior multiple.

The bear's rebuttal is that the multiple should compress — that AI structurally lowers the durable growth rate and pricing power, so a low-teens P/E is the new normal, not a discount. That is the debate in one sentence. Our base case gives partial credit to both: we re-rate to ~16× FY27E (still below the historical range) for ~$440, above neither an aggressive re-rating nor a permanent-impairment view. Street targets (context): consensus $453, high $720, low $275 (the low equals today's price — one desk sees no upside, another sees +160%). The unusually wide spread is the honest signal: conviction is low across the board, and ours is too. Not a value trap on the numbers; a value call contingent on the moat.

8. Moat & competitive position

Intuit's moat is a classic software triple: (1) switching costs — QuickBooks is the system of record for millions of small businesses; migrating your books is painful, and accountants are trained on it; (2) brand + habit in consumer tax — TurboTax is the default DIY filing product for a huge installed base; (3) data + distribution flywheel — Credit Karma's ~100M+ member base feeds a lead-gen engine, and cross-sell (payments, payroll, capital, Mailchimp) deepens ARPU. Returns on capital (23% ROE, 16% ROIC) are moat evidence.

The threat is specific and current: generative and agentic AI could (a) let free/cheap tools do simple returns and bookkeeping, compressing the DIY-tax and entry-level QuickBooks tiers, and (b) commoditize the software layer generally. Intuit's counter is to be the AI layer (Intuit Assist) and lean on data + compliance + integration that a raw model lacks. Unresolved — hence the multiple.

Peer set (FMP-supplied, market cap): the list FMP returns is a loose Nasdaq-100 tech basket — Salesforce $136B, ServiceNow $110B, Shopify $155B, Uber $152B, AppLovin $177B, plus semis (AMAT, LRCX, QCOM, ANET, APH). The relevant comps are the application-software names (CRM, NOW, SHOP): against those, INTU's ~10× forward P/E is a steep discount to ServiceNow and Salesforce, reflecting the AI-disruption discount the market is applying specifically to Intuit's tax/SMB exposure.

9. Management, capital allocation & guidance

  • Capital allocation: shareholder-friendly and disciplined. FY25 returned ~$2.77B in buybacks and $1.19B in dividends (dividend $4.64/sh, ~1.7% yield, payout ~29%) against $6.08B FCF — comfortably covered, with room to keep repurchasing at a depressed price. Asset-light (capex <1% of revenue). M&A has been strategic (Credit Karma, Mailchimp) and mostly digested; goodwill+intangibles are ~$19B, so watch for impairment risk if segment growth disappoints.
  • Insider activity — a genuine bright spot: amid routine Rule-10b5-1 director sales (director Dalzell selling small tranches through June 2026), director Vasant Prabhu made open-market purchases — 1,250 shares at ~$309 (2026-05-22) and 500 more at ~$310 (2026-05-26), roughly $540K of discretionary buying near the lows. Open-market insider buying is rare and higher-signal than programmatic selling; it is a modest but real vote of confidence.
  • Guidance / SBC caveat: management guides to non-GAAP metrics; stock-based comp (~$1.97B, ~10% of revenue) is a real economic cost the non-GAAP EPS excludes. We flag this so the 10× "forward P/E" is read honestly — on GAAP the multiple is higher, though still moderate for the quality.

11. Key risks

  • AI disruption (structural, and the whole debate): free/agentic AI doing taxes and bookkeeping could commoditize the core and erode pricing power — the exact fear that took the stock from ~$800 to $275 (compound_and_friends-I601uZxpNoM:d15d50650e frames INTU as the "software is dead from AI" poster child). If the bears are right, the low multiple is deserved, not a discount.
  • Value-trap risk: a cheap multiple can stay cheap (or fall further) if the durable growth rate has genuinely stepped down. The technicals (−66% drawdown, death cross) show the market is not yet convinced.
  • Seasonality / single-season dependence: the fiscal Q3 tax quarter is ~40% of the year; a weak tax season swings the whole result.
  • Regulatory: IRS Direct File (free government filing) and any FTC/advertising scrutiny of TurboTax "free" marketing are ongoing overhangs on the Consumer segment.
  • Thin expert coverage: with a single KB claim, we have little independent conviction to lean on — this verdict is quant/fundamentals-driven and sized accordingly.

12. Verdict, position sizing & monitoring

Buy — Tactical. Intuit is an elite software franchise (81% gross margin, 23% ROE, $6B FCF, ~14% forward EPS growth) trading at ~10× forward earnings and a 10% free-cash-flow yield because the market fears AI commoditizes tax and bookkeeping. The fundamentals say the fear is overdone; the single available expert voice agrees; a director bought stock near the lows. But expert breadth is thin (one claim), the chart is a falling knife (−66%, death cross), and the AI threat is real and unresolved — so this is a contrarian value/quality bet, not a core holding.

  • Sizing: satellite, ~1–3% of the flagship, scaled in — a starter now, adds on evidence the moat is holding (QBO growth, AI monetization) or on a technical turn (reclaim the 50-DMA). Do not lump into a falling knife.
  • Monitoring: re-underwrite on the §10 tripwires; formal re-score each earnings print, with the FY27 guide (2026-08-20) as the next hard checkpoint. This verdict is logged as a tracked Synthos call as of 2026-07-03 at $275.35.
  • Single biggest risk: AI genuinely commoditizing DIY tax and entry-level bookkeeping — if that happens, the cheap multiple is the correct multiple.
