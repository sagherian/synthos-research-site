The Overview

Navitas makes advanced power-conversion chips out of two newer materials — gallium nitride (GaN) and silicon carbide (SiC) — that waste less energy than ordinary silicon when converting electricity. That matters a lot for AI datacenters: NVIDIA's next-generation racks will draw so much power (up to a megawatt each) that the industry is redesigning how electricity gets into them, moving to an 800-volt architecture. NVIDIA publicly named Navitas as one of the chipmakers helping build that — and the stock exploded on the news, from about $5-6 to as high as $34.

Here is the problem: being named is not the same as being paid. NVIDIA named at least 14 chip companies to the same program, including giants like Infineon and Texas Instruments. Nobody — not NVIDIA, not Navitas, not any analyst we can find — has said how many dollars of Navitas chips go into each rack, or whether Navitas wins any socket at all. Meanwhile the actual business shrank: sales nearly halved last year to $46M, the company currently loses money on every chip it sells (negative gross margin in two of the last three quarters), it burns roughly $25M a quarter running the company, and it has paid for that by issuing new shares — 53% more shares exist now than in 2022. Wall Street's own forecasts say 2026 sales will be lower than 2025. And company insiders, including the CEO and a director, sold millions of dollars of stock near the top in May 2026 (SEC Form 4 filings).

Our three scores in everyday terms:

  • Downside Risk 8/10 (high). Money-losing at the gross-margin line, hugely volatile (beta ~3.8), diluting shareholders, already down 64% from its high — the cash pile (~$230M) is the only cushion.
  • Growth Quality 1/10 (none). There is no growth to grade — revenue is shrinking. Everything positive about this story is a forecast, not a result.
  • Exponential Potential 5/10 (real but unpriceable). If Navitas wins meaningful 800V sockets, the upside is genuinely large (forecasts reach $500M by 2030). But it's an if with no numbers behind it yet.

The one big thing to remember: the stock already did the round trip — $5.44 to $34.17 and back to $11.46 inside a year. The market has voted twice on this story. Until sales actually inflect, price is attention, not value.

Putting a number on it: our fair-value estimate is $7.50 against a current price of $11.46 — consistent with our call to stay away or wait for a better setup.

1. What it is

Navitas Semiconductor (Nasdaq: NVTS) designs and sells wide-bandgap power semiconductors: GaNFast gallium-nitride power ICs, GeneSiC silicon-carbide devices, plus silicon system controllers and isolators, for power conversion and charging (FMP profile). End markets span mobile/consumer fast chargers (its original volume business), solar, EV/automotive, and — the entirety of the current story — AI datacenter power. Founded 2014, Torrance CA, ~190 employees, CEO Chris Allexandre; came public via SPAC on 2021-10-20. It is fabless and tiny relative to the power-semi incumbents (Infineon, onsemi, ST, TI).

The story that moved the stock: on May 21, 2025 NVIDIA selected Navitas (8-K filed; EDGAR CIK 1821769) to co-develop the 800 V HVDC power architecture supporting Kyber rack-scale systems and Rubin Ultra — 1 MW IT racks — using both GaNFast and GeneSiC. Navitas was honored at NVIDIA's Partner Ceremony in Taipei (May 29, 2026), and at GTC 2026 (March) and Computex 2026 demonstrated a single-stage 800V→6V GaN power-delivery board (16× 650V GaNFast FETs) that eliminates the 48V intermediate bus, with claimed peak efficiency of 96.5% (March) rising to 97.5% (June) at 1 MHz and 2,100 W/in³ power density. Those are demo-board targets, not shipping product, and not third-party validated. The essential context: NVIDIA's 800 VDC partner list names at least 14 silicon companies — Infineon, TI, onsemi, ST, MPS, ROHM, ADI, Renesas, Innoscience, AOS, EPC, Power Integrations, Richtek, and Navitas — so being named is table stakes for the category, not a moat. (All facts this paragraph: Synthos verified-research run, 2026-07-19, sources as cited.)

Revenue mix: FMP segment data shows a single reportable segment ($83.3M, FY2024 — the last year broken out); the company does not disclose a datacenter/800V revenue line at all, which is itself a tell. Geography (per profile): US, Europe, China, rest of Asia — with China both a major end market and, via Innoscience, its most dangerous competitor.

4. Exponential Potential

Synthos separates compounders (durable returns on real demand) from exponentials (accelerating, multi-baggers-from-here). Navitas is a pure option on an exponential, with no compounder underneath:

  • Forward growth (all estimates): consensus revenue $42.4M (2026E) → $500M (2030E) — on paper a spectacular CAGR. But 2026E is below 2025 actuals, 2030E rests on 6 revenue analysts (2 for EPS) and assumes the 800V ramp Navitas has not yet quantified its share of. EBITDA is modeled negative through 2029E; the first positive net income year is 2030E (~$68M avg, EPS ~$0.33 — 2 analysts).
  • Acceleration (the 2nd derivative) is currently negative: trailing revenue is decelerating into contraction (−44.9% FY25, −39% YoY in Q1-26). The inflection exists only in the out-year estimate columns. The flagship hunts confirmed acceleration; this is the opposite.
  • Room to run (the honest bull point): 1 MW racks and 800 VDC distribution are real, NVIDIA has committed the architecture publicly, and wide-bandgap content per rack grows for a decade. If Navitas converts its GaN+SiC portfolio into named sockets with disclosed dollar content, the 2030E path is credible and a ~$2.8B cap could re-rate. No public source today lets anyone underwrite that share.
  • Reinvestment runway: funded by shareholders, not operations — FCF −$44.4M (FY25), with $202.5M of equity issued in 2025. The model literally requires the story to stay hot enough to sell stock into.

Exponential Potential: Moderate (5/10). A genuine call option on the 800V transition — but an option is what it is, and the market is charging ~60-80× sales for it after two failed breakouts.

8. Moat & competitive position

Navitas's claim to a moat is technology breadth in wide-bandgap power — one of few pure-plays with both GaN (GaNFast, monolithic integration) and SiC (GeneSiC) under one roof, and demo-level performance (97.5% claimed peak efficiency, 2,100 W/in³ on the 800V→6V board — company figures, not third-party validated). That is genuine engineering. It is not yet a business moat, for four reasons: (1) the partner list is the anti-moat — NVIDIA named 14+ silicon companies to the same 800 VDC program, including Infineon, TI, onsemi, ST, MPS, ROHM, ADI, Renesas, Innoscience, AOS, EPC, Power Integrations and Richtek; several have vastly greater scale, automotive-grade quality systems, and existing NVIDIA/hyperscaler relationships; (2) no quantified content — no source anywhere breaks out per-rack power-semi dollar content or Navitas's share of it; (3) trailing-edge economics — power semis are not leading-edge logic; Dylan Patel's KB-tracked claims (2026-02-03/05, thematic context — his text names TI/ST, not Navitas) describe China power chips "matching and undercutting" Western incumbents, with Innoscience the China GaN volume leader; (4) customer concentration of narrative — the equity story is one customer's architecture slide.

Peer set: FMP's peer list is noisy (it includes Grindr and Quantum Computing — ignore those). The relevant comps are Power Integrations (POWI, ~$3.9B cap) — profitable, at a fraction of NVTS's sales multiple — plus the diversified power incumbents (Infineon, onsemi, ST, TI) and private/China GaN (Innoscience, EPC). Against every one of them, Navitas is the smallest, least profitable, and most expensive per dollar of revenue.
