In plain English

Oracle used to be known for boring but hugely profitable database software that big companies and governments run their operations on. Over the last two years it has become one of the main places where AI companies (think the firms training ChatGPT-style models) rent enormous amounts of computing power. Business is booming: Oracle now has $638 billion of signed future orders on the books — more than three times what it had a year ago.

Here's the catch, and it's a big one. To fill those orders Oracle has to build giant datacenters first, which costs a fortune up front. Last year it spent about $56 billion on construction and, after that, actually burned $24 billion of cash and borrowed heavily to do it. The stock, which had roughly quadrupled, then fell by more than half — from about $328 to $140 — as investors got nervous about all that debt and spending.

Our verdict is Buy — Tactical: we think the drop has gone too far given the size of the order book, but this is a bumpy, higher-risk bet you size small, not a sleep-well-at-night core holding.

Here's what our three scores mean in everyday terms:

  • Downside Risk 7/10 (elevated — higher is riskier). Lots of debt, negative cash flow, and a stock that swings hard. If the AI buildout hits a snag, it could fall further.
  • Growth Quality 8/10 (very good). The business is growing fast with a real, signed backlog behind it — this is not hype without customers.
  • Exponential Potential 7/10 (high). The order book is not just big, it's speeding up, and the market for AI computing is enormous — that's what an exponential looks like.

The one big worry: Oracle is spending and borrowing gigantic sums to build AI datacenters before the money comes in. If those customers pay as promised, this works. If demand cools or Oracle can't fund the buildout, the debt becomes a serious problem.

1. What it is

Oracle Corporation (NYSE: ORCL) is a ~50-year-old enterprise-technology company (founded 1977, HQ Austin, TX) that has pivoted from its legacy of the Oracle Database and Fusion/NetSuite cloud applications into a leading provider of AI cloud infrastructure (OCI) — renting compute, storage, and networking at scale to AI labs and enterprises. Fiscal year ends May 31.

Revenue mix (FY2025 filings, from FMP segmentation):

  • By product line: Cloud & License $49.23B (86%) · Services $5.23B · Hardware $2.94B.
  • The AI split that matters (FY26 earnings release): Total Cloud (IaaS + SaaS) $34.0B, +39%; within that, Cloud Infrastructure (IaaS) $18.1B, +77% and Cloud Applications (SaaS) $15.9B, +11%. Legacy software $24.5B was down 1% as customers migrate to cloud. The whole growth story is the IaaS line.
  • By geography (FY25): Americas $36.3B (64%) · EMEA $14.0B (25%) · Asia-Pacific $7.0B (12%). US/Americas-weighted but genuinely global.

The strategic story the panel keeps returning to is a single pivot: Oracle is no longer priced as a software company but as a compute/scarcity name — a supplier of the scarce GPU-datacenter capacity that AI demand is outstripping (§2).

4. Exponential Potential

Synthos separates compounders (durable high returns on capital) from exponentials (accelerating, multi-baggers-from-here). ORCL is a rare mega-cap that screens as a genuine exponential — because the second derivative is positive:

  • Forward growth: revenue guided/estimated FY26 $67.4B → FY27 $90B (+34%) → FY28E $130B → FY29E $179B; non-GAAP EPS FY27 $8.05 → FY28E $10.9 → FY29E $15.4 → FY30E $19.8 → FY31E $24.0. A ~25%+ revenue CAGR and ~25%+ EPS CAGR through the decade on consensus.
  • Acceleration (the 2nd derivative) is positive: total-cloud growth is rising (IaaS +77% FY26, guided +58–64% Q1'27), and RPO exploded +363% YoY to $638B — that is the exponential's signature. Unlike a decelerating mega-cap, Oracle's growth is speeding up. Per our flagship philosophy we hunt forward next-exponentials over trailing compounders — Oracle is one of the few names in the pool where the acceleration is real and quantified.
  • Room to run: at $404B market cap against an AI-infrastructure TAM measured in the trillions, the ceiling is high; a 3× from here is a ~$1.2T company, plausible if the backlog converts. Room to run is not the binding constraint — funding is.
  • The honest asterisk: the thing separating Oracle from a clean 9 is that the exponential is being bought with debt and negative free cash flow. The backlog is the accelerant; the −$23.7B FCF and $125B net debt are the drag. A pure-play with this backlog and a fortress balance sheet would score 9; Oracle scores 7 because the cash story is unresolved.

Exponential Potential: High (7/10). Own it for a genuinely accelerating AI-compute backlog into a vast TAM — but with eyes open that the multibagger case requires the FCF hole to close.

5. Financials (real numbers — FMP annual + FY26 earnings release)

  • Revenue: FY26 (ended May 2026) $67.36B, +17.4% (FY25 $57.4B, +8.4% on FY24 $52.96B). The mix shift is the story: cloud infrastructure +77%, legacy software −1%.
  • Quarterly trajectory (re-acceleration): Q1'26 $14.93B → Q2 $16.06B → Q3 $17.19B → Q4'26 $19.18B (+21% YoY). Sequential acceleration through the latest print.
  • The crown jewel — RPO: Remaining Performance Obligations ended FY26 at $638B, up 363% YoY and +$85B sequentially. Most of the increase is large-scale AI contracts, several prepaid or with customer-supplied GPUs (~$75B) — which materially de-risks the funding (management's framing; see §9).
  • Margins: gross 65.8% TTM, EBITDA margin 47.7%, operating ~30.8%, net 25.4% TTM. Solid, but note gross margin is lower than a pure-software company because IaaS carries datacenter/depreciation cost.
  • Earnings: FY26 net income $17.09B (+37%); GAAP EPS $5.83, non-GAAP $7.63. Q4'26 GAAP EPS $1.45. ROE an eye-catching 50.6% (flattered by thin equity/high leverage).
  • Cash flow — the crux: operating CF $32.0B (+54%), but capex −$55.7B → free cash flow −$23.7B. Oracle is spending far more than it earns to build AI capacity. Capex/revenue is ~83%. This is the single number the bear case lives on — watch it inflect.
  • Balance sheet: total debt $156B, net debt $124.9B, net-debt/EBITDA 3.9× (up from ~2.0× two years ago — leverage is climbing to fund the buildout). Interest expense $4.6B/yr; interest coverage ~4.5×. Investment-grade but no longer conservative. FMP's letter rating is C+ (DCF and debt-to-equity sub-scores at the floor) — a quant flag we take seriously.

6. Valuation — priced in or washed out?

On the forward earnings Oracle has guided to, the stock is no longer expensive after the crash: 17× FY27E non-GAAP EPS ($8.05), ~13× FY28E ($10.9), ~9× FY29E ($15.4) — cheap for a 25%+ grower if you believe the estimates. Trailing it is 24× GAAP EPS / 18× non-GAAP, EV/S 7.9×, EV/EBITDA 16.5×. The PEG (forward) sits at ~0.67 — screening outright cheap on growth.

The catch every valuation must confront: you cannot value Oracle on earnings alone while FCF is −$23.7B. The reverse-DCF read is that at $140 the market is pricing meaningful doubt that the backlog converts to cash — the multiple compression from ~9× sales at the highs to ~6× sales now is the market front-running the funding risk. Our base FV of ~$205 applies ~24× to FY28E power discounted back — constructive versus the depressed price but a haircut to the Street's $253.5 because we refuse to wave through the cash-flow hole. Street targets (context): consensus $253.5, high $325, low $160 — the entire published range sits above today's $140, which tells you the sell-side sees the drawdown as overdone; we agree directionally but size for the risk they underweight. Not a value stock in the classic sense; a high-growth AI-cloud leader on sale, with a real balance-sheet asterisk.

8. Moat & competitive position

Oracle's moat is a two-parter: (1) the legacy database + Fusion/NetSuite install base — mission-critical, high-switching-cost enterprise software that throws off the cash funding the pivot; and (2) an emerging AI-infrastructure position where the edge is scarce, buildable capacity, GPU-supply relationships (Nvidia), and multi-cloud database placement. The competitive frame is brutal: OCI competes with AWS, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud — all with fortress balance sheets and their own AI capex. Oracle's differentiation is speed of buildout, price, and willingness to sign huge single-customer contracts (the OpenAI deal). Threats: hyperscaler retaliation on price, customer concentration in a few mega-AI contracts, and the funding disadvantage vs. cash-rich rivals.

Peer set (FMP-supplied, mixed relevance). FMP's peer list is a grab-bag of infrastructure-software names rather than true cloud comps: SAP $189B, ASML $682B, Palantir $297B, Fortinet $114B, Adobe $87B, Synopsys $84B, plus small-caps (Wix, Radware, i3 Verticals, Helport AI). The economically relevant comparison is the hyperscalers (AWS/Azure/GCP) — not in this list. Read the peer table as sector context, not a valuation comp set.

9. Management, capital allocation & guidance

  • Capital allocation: all-in on the AI buildout — $55.7B capex FY26, funded by $43B debt + $5B equity raised. Dividend maintained ($0.50/qtr, declared for July 2026); buybacks essentially paused ($206M). This is an aggressive, high-conviction, leveraged growth bet — appropriate only if the backlog is real, which is the whole debate.
  • Insider activity: the most recent Form 4s (filed 2026-06-26) show Vice Chairman Jeffrey Henley exercising options and selling ~344k shares at $156–162 into the drawdown. Read as routine option-exercise/diversification by a long-tenured insider, but worth noting it is selling, not buying, on the dip — no insider is stepping in to catch the knife in the sampled window.
  • Management's own guidance (half-weighted — they talk their book). The FY26 Q4 earnings release (SEC 8-K, 2026-06-10) is a real earnings release and gives dated forward guidance, summarized here as management's self-interested words:

- Q1'27: total revenue +27–29%; total cloud +58–64% USD; non-GAAP EPS $1.72–$1.76 (+17–20%).

- Full FY27: confirmed $90B total revenue; raised non-GAAP EPS guidance to $8.05 (+18% adjusted for one-time FY26 items).

- Funding: plans to raise ~$40B in FY27 (debt + equity, incl. a $20B at-the-market equity program); no additional debt expected in calendar 2026. The ATM equity is a dilution flag.

- RPO framing: ~$75B of the AI backlog is prepaid or customer-supplied GPUs, which management says "substantially reduces the amount of capital Oracle must raise." Credible and important — but it is management's framing, so half-weighted.

11. Key risks

  • Balance-sheet / FCF (the dominant risk): FCF −$23.7B, net debt $125B, net-debt/EBITDA 3.9× and rising, ~$40B of FY27 funding still to raise. Oracle is building ahead of the cash. No cited expert voice underwrites this — it is our own flag and the reason risk scores 7.
  • Customer / contract concentration: a large share of the RPO surge is a handful of mega AI contracts (the $300B OpenAI deal looms large). Concentration cuts both ways — one cancellation is material.
  • Competitive: AWS/Azure/GCP are better-capitalized and can compete on price; Oracle is the leveraged challenger, not the incumbent leader.
  • Valuation-on-estimates risk: the stock looks cheap only on forward numbers that assume the buildout works. If RPO doesn't convert, the "cheap" multiple was on phantom earnings.
  • Volatility / drawdown: beta 1.66 and a live −57% drawdown; this can keep falling before it turns. RSI 15 is oversold, not a bottom guarantee.
