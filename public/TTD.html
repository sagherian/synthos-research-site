In plain English

The Trade Desk runs the "buy side" of internet advertising: it's the software big brands and their agencies use to buy ads automatically across websites, streaming TV (think ad-supported Netflix/Roku), audio, and apps — everywhere except the walled gardens like Google, Meta, and Amazon's own properties. It makes money by taking a cut of the ad spending that flows through its platform, and it keeps a very high share of each dollar as profit.

Here's the story: this was one of the market's darling growth stocks, and then it broke. The stock is down about 75% in the past year because its sales stopped growing as fast as investors expected — growth slid from roughly 40% a year to about 12% — and because Amazon is now muscling into the same business. After that crash, the stock is no longer expensive; it's actually priced reasonably for the first time in years, and the company has more cash than debt and generates real free cash.

Our verdict is Watch — meaning it's interesting but we're not buying yet. The business is good and the price is fair, but when a growth company's growth is still slowing and a giant competitor is circling, the smart move is to wait for proof that growth has stopped falling before stepping in.

Here's what our three scores mean in everyday terms:

  • Downside Risk 6/10 (a bit above average). The balance sheet is safe (net cash), but the stock has already proven it can fall a lot, growth is slipping, and Amazon is a real threat.
  • Growth Quality 6/10 (decent, not great). Very profitable and cash-rich, but the once-torrid growth has cooled sharply and profit margins are slipping.
  • Exponential Potential 4/10 (low-moderate). The market it plays in is big, but the fast acceleration that made this a rocket is gone — it's growing at a normal pace now.

The one big worry: Amazon's ad-buying tool and the walled gardens are attacking exactly the open-internet turf The Trade Desk depends on, right as its growth is already slowing.

1. What it is

The Trade Desk (NASDAQ: TTD) is a global ad-tech company founded in 2009 and headquartered in Ventura, California. It operates a self-service, cloud-based demand-side platform (DSP) — the software that advertising buyers (mostly agencies and their brand clients) use to plan, buy, and optimize data-driven digital ad campaigns programmatically across display, video, connected TV (CTV), audio, native, and social, reaching users on desktop, mobile, and streaming devices. TTD is the largest independent DSP — it does not own media, which is its core "we're on your side, not conflicted" pitch versus Google and Amazon. Fiscal year ends December 31. ~3,522 employees. Founder-CEO Jeff Green.

Its strategic assets/initiatives: Unified ID 2.0 (UID2), an industry identity standard positioned as the open-internet answer to cookie deprecation; OpenPath, direct supply-side integrations with publishers; Kokai/Koa, its AI-driven optimization layer (now extended to "Koa Agents," agentic AI for media buying); and a heavy CTV push as streaming ad budgets shift online.

Revenue mix:

  • By geography (FY2025, from filings): United States $2.477B (85%) · Non-US $0.420B (15%). Heavily US-weighted; international is the stated long-term growth lever but remains small.
  • By product/segment: FMP provides no product-level segmentation — TTD reports as a single platform business (take-rate on gross ad spend), so there is no drug-style franchise breakout.

4. Exponential Potential

Synthos separates compounders (durable high returns on capital) from exponentials (accelerating, multi-baggers-from-here). TTD is a former exponential that has downshifted to a decelerating compounder:

  • Forward growth: revenue CAGR FY25→FY30E ~9.9% ($2.90B → $4.64B, est.); EPS CAGR ~17.4% ($0.91 → $2.04 est.) as buybacks and modest margin help compound the bottom line faster than the top.
  • Acceleration (the 2nd derivative) is decisively negative: revenue growth was ~40%+ in the 2021–2023 boom, +18.5% FY25, then +12% in Q1'26 — and consensus models ~10% FY26E. The growth engine has cooled dramatically. This is the single most important fact about the stock and the reason the "exponential" label no longer fits.
  • Room to run: the open-internet + CTV programmatic TAM is genuinely large (tens of billions of ad dollars still shifting to streaming and the open web), so demand runway exists. But TAM alone doesn't make an exponential — you need acceleration into that TAM, and TTD's is negative right now, with Amazon contesting the very share it needs to capture.
  • Reinvestment runway: healthy — R&D is ~18% of revenue, capex ~9%, and FCF is strongly positive ($796M FY25), so TTD can self-fund its AI/CTV push. The constraint is competitive, not financial.

Exponential Potential: Low-Moderate (4/10). Own TTD, if at all, for high-margin cash generation and a possible growth re-acceleration — not for a fast multibagger. A re-rating requires TTD to prove growth has bottomed; until then the honest read is a decelerating mid-cap, not a rocket.

5. Financials (real numbers — FMP annual/quarterly)

  • Revenue: FY25 $2.896B, +18.5% (FY24 $2.445B, +25.6% on FY23 $1.946B). Still growing, but the rate is falling fast: FY23 +21% → FY24 +26% → FY25 +18.5% → Q1'26 +12% YoY.
  • Quarterly trajectory (the deceleration, in real numbers): Q1'25 $616M (+25% YoY) → Q2 $694M → Q3 $739M → Q4 $847M → Q1'26 $689M (+12% YoY). Year-over-year growth roughly halved in four quarters — the core problem.
  • Margins: gross 77.8% TTM (strong and stable), EBITDA 26.5% TTM, operating ~20%, net 14.6% TTM. But margins are slipping: Q1'26 adj-EBITDA margin fell to 30% from 34% a year earlier (per the earnings release), a sign TTD is spending to defend growth.
  • Earnings: net income $443M FY25 (up from $393M FY24); EPS $0.91–0.92. Q1'26 net income just $40M (6% net margin) vs $51M a year earlier — GAAP profit went down year-over-year, pressured by ~$490M/yr stock-based comp (~16% of revenue — a real dilution/cost flag).
  • Cash flow: operating CF $993M, capex −$197M, FCF $796M FY25 (9.3% FCF yield) — the genuine bright spot. Cash conversion is excellent.
  • Balance sheet: cash & ST investments $1.30B, total debt $436M (mostly capital leases), net debt −$222M (net cash), net-debt/EBITDA −0.58×. Current ratio 1.68×. Fortress-clean — no solvency risk.
  • Buybacks: TTD repurchased $1.38B of stock in FY25 and ~$164M in Q1'26 — aggressive, and at least partly offsetting the heavy SBC dilution.

On TTM it trades at 21× earnings, 2.9× sales, 11× EV/EBITDA, 10.7× price/FCF (9.3% FCF yield) — a far cry from the 20×+ sales it commanded at the peak. On forward consensus the multiple compresses further: ~19× FY26E EPS ($1.00) → ~16× FY27E ($1.20) → ~9× FY30E ($2.04) if estimates hit. A stock down ~75% has done most of the de-rating work; the debate now is whether earnings hold and grow into that cheaper multiple, or whether decelerating growth + Amazon competition force estimates lower (the value-trap risk). 8. Moat & competitive position

TTD's moat is being the largest independent DSP in a market where the alternatives (Google DV360, Amazon DSP) are conflicted — they own media and sit on both sides of the auction. TTD's neutrality, its scale of buyer relationships (>95% customer retention for over a decade, per the Q1'26 release), and its identity/data infrastructure (UID2, OpenPath, Kokai/Koa AI) are real durable assets. CTV — where TTD has premium inventory partnerships across streaming — is the growth engine and the clearest competitive edge.

But the moat is under active assault: (1) Amazon's DSP is scaling fast, leveraging Amazon's shopper data and retail-media flywheel to attack the same open-internet budgets; (2) the walled gardens (Google, Meta, Amazon-owned) keep capturing the majority of digital ad dollars; (3) cookie deprecation / identity fragmentation threatens the targeting TTD monetizes, which is exactly why UID2 matters. The deceleration in growth is the market's verdict that the competitive pressure is biting.

Peer set (FMP-listed, market cap): the FMP peer list is poor for TTD — it names Figma $10B, Flex $50B, Grab $15B, Jabil $36B, Strategy $30B, PTC $14B, SS&C $16B, Tyler Technologies $13B, VeriSign $23B, Zoom $26B — none are true ad-tech comps. The real competitive frame is Google (Alphabet), Amazon (DSP), Meta (walled garden), and smaller ad-tech peers (Magnite, PubMatic, Criteo, AppLovin on the buy/supply spectrum). Judge TTD against those, not the FMP list.

9. Management, capital allocation & guidance

  • Capital allocation: shareholder-friendly but SBC-heavy. TTD pays no dividend, reinvests in R&D (~18% of revenue), and runs a large buyback ($1.38B FY25; $327M authorization remaining after Q1'26) — partly to offset ~$490M/yr stock-based comp (~16% of revenue), which is a genuine dilution/quality caveat. Founder-CEO Jeff Green remains at the helm.
  • Insider activity: the sampled Form 4s (through 2026-06-15) show routine director stock awards and some tax-withholding ("F-InKind") dispositions plus one director open-market sale (~53.7k shares at $21.14, 2026-05-28). No alarming cluster of discretionary insider selling in the window; mostly comp-mechanics.
  • Management's own guidance (half-weighted — self-interested): From the SEC-filed Q1'26 8-K (2026-05-07) earnings release, management guided Q2'26 revenue "at least $750 million" and Adjusted EBITDA "of approximately $260 million." CEO Jeff Green framed Q1 as "another strong quarter" with 12% growth and >95% customer retention, and highlighted new AI ("Koa Agents"), OpenAds/OpenPath publisher adoption, LinkedIn selecting TTD as its first DSP partner for B2B CTV data, and continued UID2 momentum. Weight this as management's own book (half-weight): the ≥$750M Q2 revenue guide implies only ~8% YoY growth (vs $694M in Q2'25), quietly confirming the deceleration even in management's optimistic framing. Note TTD declines to guide GAAP net income, citing SBC/share-price variability.

11. Key risks

  • Decelerating growth (the core risk): growth halved (from ~25% to ~12% YoY) in a year; if it keeps sliding, even the cheaper multiple is a trap.
  • Amazon DSP + walled gardens (structural): the biggest secular threat — Amazon's data/retail-media flywheel and Google/Meta's scale directly contest TTD's open-internet turf.
  • Identity / cookie deprecation: fragmenting targeting signals threaten the data-driven value TTD sells; UID2 adoption is the mitigant but not guaranteed.
  • Macro ad-spend cyclicality: advertising is discretionary and cyclical; a downturn hits programmatic budgets first.
  • Stock-based comp / dilution: ~$490M/yr SBC (~16% of revenue) flatters non-GAAP profitability and pressures GAAP EPS; buybacks are partly just offsetting it.
  • No expert conviction: zero KB coverage means no independent-panel signal either way — this call leans entirely on quant + fundamentals.

12. Verdict, position sizing & monitoring

Watch. The Trade Desk is a genuinely good business — 78% gross margins, $796M FCF, net cash, >95% retention, and the leading independent DSP — trading at a reasonable valuation (21× earnings, 9.3% FCF yield) for the first time in years after a ~75% drawdown. But the two things that would turn "cheap and good" into "buy" are missing: growth is still decelerating (12% and falling) and a formidable competitor (Amazon) is attacking the core market — and there is no expert conviction in our KB to lean on. The prudent stance is to wait for evidence that growth has bottomed before committing.

  • Sizing: Watch-list; if you must own a starter, keep it ≤1–2% and treat it as a turnaround option, not a core position. Add only on confirmation (growth stabilizing + price reclaiming the 50-DMA).
  • Monitoring: re-underwrite on the §10 tripwires; formal re-score at the 2026-08-06 print. This verdict is logged as a tracked Synthos call as of 2026-07-03 at $19.10.
  • Single biggest risk: Amazon's DSP and the walled gardens taking open-internet share while TTD's growth is already slowing — the scenario that turns a cheap stock into a value trap.
