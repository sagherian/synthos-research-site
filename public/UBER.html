In plain English

Uber runs the app that connects riders with drivers (Mobility), hungry people with restaurants and stores (Delivery), and shippers with truckers (Freight). It is the biggest of its kind in most of the world. The business is now solidly profitable and throws off a lot of cash — it made about $9.8 billion of free cash flow last year and is buying back its own stock.

The stock is moderately cheap for the quality — it has actually fallen about 26% from its high while the overall market rose, so you are paying less than usual for a growing, cash-generating leader. Our verdict is Buy — Tactical: a good business at a fair-to-cheap price, but with one large unresolved question hanging over it, so size it as a smaller "satellite" holding, not a cornerstone.

Here's what our three scores mean in everyday terms:

  • Downside Risk 5/10 (middle). Low debt and gushing cash make it sturdy, but the stock swings more than the market and one big question (self-driving cars) could reset the whole story.
  • Growth Quality 8/10 (very good). Growing fast for its size, high returns on the money invested, and a real network advantage — more riders attract more drivers, and vice versa.
  • Exponential Potential 6/10 (moderate-high). Bookings are still growing ~20% a year and self-driving could open a huge new market, but at $151 billion it is already big, so a quick 5-bagger is unlikely.

The one big worry: self-driving cars ("robotaxis"). The optimists think Uber becomes the place all those robo-cars go to find riders. The pessimists think the robo-car makers (or Tesla, Waymo) skip Uber entirely. Nobody knows yet — and that single question is why the stock is cheap.

1. What it is

Uber Technologies (NYSE: UBER) is the global ride-hailing, food/grocery delivery, and freight-brokerage platform — a two-sided marketplace that matches consumers with independent drivers and merchants. Founded 2009, IPO'd 2019, run by CEO Dara Khosrowshahi. Fiscal year ends December 31. The economic engine is a take-rate on gross bookings: in Q1'26, $53.7B of gross bookings converted to $13.2B of revenue. The strategic anchor now is Uber One membership (50M members, ~half of all gross bookings) plus a "capital-light" approach to autonomous vehicles — partnering with AV makers rather than building the cars.

Revenue mix (FY2025, FMP product segmentation — clean revenue basis):

  • Mobility $29.67B (57%) — the profit engine; ride-hailing take-rate.
  • Delivery $17.25B (33%) — Eats/grocery; now solidly profitable at the segment line.
  • Freight $5.10B (10%) — low-margin brokerage, roughly flat-to-shrinking; the drag on the story.

(Note: FMP's geographic segmentation table for FY25 appears to mix a gross-bookings basis into the US/EMEA lines and is internally inconsistent with the $52B revenue total, so we rely on the product segmentation above and management's disclosed geographic revenue. Uber remains US/Canada-led with fast-growing international Mobility.)

Segment operating income (Q1'26, from the earnings release): Mobility $2.03B, Delivery $0.96B, Freight −$0.03B — Mobility is the cash cow, Delivery has crossed into real profit, Freight is a rounding error.

4. Exponential Potential

Synthos separates compounders (durable high returns on capital) from exponentials (accelerating multi-baggers-from-here). UBER is a high-quality compounder with a real but two-sided exponential option:

  • Forward growth: consensus revenue CAGR FY25→FY30E ~12% ($52B → $92B); EPS CAGR FY26E→FY30E ~21% ($3.29 → $6.96). The underlying engine — constant-currency gross bookings — grew 21% in Q1'26, faster than reported revenue (which was dragged to +14% by business-model/accounting changes).
  • Acceleration (2nd derivative) is mixed: reported revenue growth is decelerating (+18% FY25 → ~+12% FY26E) partly on optical business-model changes, but gross-bookings growth has held ~21% for three straight quarters and Adjusted EBITDA is compounding ~33% — faster than the top line. So earnings are accelerating even as reported revenue slows. Honest read: not a clean accelerator, but not decelerating on the metrics that matter.
  • Room to run: the AV/physical-AI TAM is genuinely enormous, and Uber's demand density is a real asset if the marketplace thesis holds. But at $151B a 5× implies a ~$755B company — plausible over a decade only if AV breaks Uber's way. The optionality is real; it is also the risk.
  • Reinvestment runway: capital-light — capex is only ~$0.34B against $10B operating cash flow, so incremental growth is nearly free-cash-flow accretive. This is the underrated strength.

Exponential Potential: Moderate-High (6). Own it for high-teens/low-20s earnings compounding at a capital-light platform, plus a genuine (double-edged) AV call option — not for a guaranteed multibagger. The $151B cap and the two-sidedness of the AV bet are why this is a 6, not an 8.

5. Financials (real numbers — FMP annual/quarterly)

  • Revenue: FY25 $52.02B, +18.3% (FY24 $43.98B, +18% on FY23 $37.28B). Consistent high-teens growth at scale.
  • Quarterly trajectory: Q1'25 $11.53B → Q2 $12.65B → Q3 $13.47B → Q4 $14.37B → Q1'26 $13.20B (+14.5% YoY; the sequential dip is seasonal + business-model change). Gross bookings grew 21% cc.
  • Margins: gross 41.0% TTM, operating ~11.7% TTM and rising, net 15.9% TTM. Adjusted EBITDA margin on gross bookings hit 4.6% in Q1'26 (up from 4.4%).
  • Earnings (read carefully): FY25 GAAP net income $10.05B, EPS diluted $4.73 — but this was flattered by a ~$4.35B deferred-tax benefit (a valuation-allowance release), so it overstates run-rate earnings power. Q1'26 GAAP net income was just $263M because of a $1.5B pre-tax equity-revaluation headwind. Underlying: Non-GAAP EPS $0.72 in Q1'26, +44% YoY. Use the estimate path, not trailing GAAP.
  • Cash flow (the clean tell): FY25 operating CF $10.1B, capex just −$0.34B, FCF $9.76B (FCF yield ~6.5%). This is the least noisy proof of earnings power. Q1'26 FCF $2.3B.
  • Balance sheet: total debt $12.1B, cash+ST investments $7.6B, net debt ~$5.0B, net-debt/EBITDA ~1.1×. Investment-grade; FMP letter rating A−. The company repurchased $6.5B of stock in FY25 — a capital-return inflection.

6. Valuation — priced in or room?

On trailing GAAP UBER looks like ~18× earnings, but that number is unreliable (tax-benefit inflated). The honest lenses:

  • Forward P/E: 23× FY26E → 17× FY27E → 14× FY28E → 11× FY30E — the multiple compresses fast if the EPS path is hit.
  • Cash-flow lens (the cleanest): P/FCF ~15×, FCF yield ~6.5% — genuinely reasonable for a high-teens grower with a capital-light model.
  • EV lenses: EV/Sales 3.0×, EV/EBITDA 26× (EBITDA understates true economics because SBC and D&A treatment; the FCF lens is fairer).
  • PEG: forward PEG ~0.5 (FMP) — cheap on a growth-adjusted basis.

Street targets (context): consensus $101.24, high $125, low $72; 50 Buy / 11 Hold / 0 Sell. Our $88 base is below consensus deliberately — we discount for the AV question rather than underwrite the Street's optimism. This is not a rich stock; the discount is the market pricing the disruption doubt, and the FCF yield gives real support under it.

8. Moat & competitive position

Uber's moat is a two-sided network effect with scale density: more riders shorten wait times and raise driver earnings, which attracts more drivers, which shortens waits further — reinforced by Uber One (50M members, ~half of bookings) and a multi-vertical super-app (rides + eats + grocery). The panel's durability claim (we_study_billionaires-_uh3A25qS10:f26c066328) rests on the ~$50B revenue / ~$8B FCF scale being hard to replicate. The threat vector is unusual: the disruptor and the moat-widener are the same technology (AVs) — hence the whole debate in §2/§11.

Peer set (FMP-supplied, market cap): the FMP peer list for UBER is its sector peers — AppLovin $177B, Shopify $155B, Salesforce $136B, ServiceNow $110B, Sony $122B, Qualcomm $186B, Applied Materials $479B, Lam Research $439B, Arista $201B, Intuit $75B — i.e. large-cap software/tech broadly, not direct mobility comps. The economically relevant competitors are Lyft (US #2), DoorDash (delivery), Waymo/Alphabet and Tesla (AV), and regional players (Grab, Didi, Bolt). Against those, Uber is the scale leader in most markets — its edge is breadth and density, not a single unassailable technology.

9. Management, capital allocation & guidance

  • Capital allocation: the inflection story — Uber turned FCF-positive and durable ($9.8B FY25), and returned $6.5B via buybacks in FY25 while keeping net-debt/EBITDA at ~1.1×. Capex is trivial (~$0.34B), so nearly all operating cash is discretionary. No dividend.
  • Insider activity: the sampled Form 4s (filing 2026-06-18) are routine officer equity vesting/tax-withholding (M-Exempt option exercises and F-InKind share surrenders at ~$73.25) by Tony West — not open-market discretionary selling. No alarming insider cluster in the window.
  • Management's own guidance (half-weighted — their book): the SEC 8-K Q1'26 earnings release (2026-05-06) is a genuine release with explicit forward guidance. For Q2'26 management guided: gross bookings $56.25B–$57.75B (18–22% YoY cc), Adjusted EBITDA $2.70B–$2.80B, and Non-GAAP EPS $0.78–$0.82 (+31–38% YoY). CEO Khosrowshahi flagged 50M Uber One members (half of bookings); CFO Krishnamurthy stressed "earnings scaling at more than twice our topline" and "a capital-efficient approach to AVs." We half-weight this — it is management talking its own book — but note it corroborates the ~20% bookings / ~30%+ EBITDA growth used in our base case.

11. Key risks

  • Autonomous vehicles (structural, two-sided): the defining risk. Bulls say Uber becomes the demand layer for AV fleets (all_in-YRQ-OG05flI:6a4761ca47); bears say Waymo/Tesla scale their own consumer apps and disintermediate Uber's marketplace. The Real Vision claim (real_vision-sKQ-Xr8rJeE:a8b6f9400f) generalizes the disintermediation worry. Unresolved — and it is why the stock is down 26%.
  • Valuation on the wrong number: trailing 18× GAAP EPS is tax-inflated; on EV/EBITDA (26×) it is not cheap. The FCF lens saves it, but a growth stumble bites.
  • Competitive/regulatory: Lyft, DoorDash, global rivals; and the perennial driver-classification (gig-worker) regulatory risk that could raise the cost base.
  • Cyclicality & beta: beta 1.12; discretionary mobility/delivery spend is somewhat consumer-cycle exposed.
  • GAAP noise: large equity-investment revaluations (a $1.5B pre-tax swing in Q1'26 alone) make headline earnings volatile and easy to misread.

12. Verdict, position sizing & monitoring

Buy — Tactical. Uber is a genuine platform compounder — FY25 revenue +18% to $52B, $9.8B FCF, ROE 33%, net-debt/EBITDA 1.1×, buying back stock — now trading at its cheapest in years (P/FCF ~15×, forward PEG ~0.5) precisely because the market can't yet resolve the AV question. The expert panel (7 net-bullish voices, 44 reconciled claims) is real but thin and AV-concentrated, and one cited voice is a disintermediation caution — so this is a Moderate-conviction, not High-conviction, call. The technicals are in a downtrend (below the 200-DMA, −19% 12-mo), which argues for tactical accumulation on weakness rather than a core lump.

  • Sizing: satellite-growth, ~2–4% of a growth sleeve. Scale in on weakness (near the 50-DMA / $70s), add on a 200-DMA reclaim, and cap the position given the binary AV overhang.
  • Monitoring: re-underwrite on the §10 tripwires; formal re-score each earnings print, with special attention to Mobility gross-bookings growth and any AV-app competitive move. This verdict is logged as a tracked Synthos call as of 2026-07-03 at $74.43.
  • Single biggest risk: autonomous vehicles — the same wave the bulls cite as the upside is the bear case, and the resolution will make or break this call.
